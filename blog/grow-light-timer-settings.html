Getting your grow light timer settings right is one of the most important factors in indoor growing success. Unlike outdoor plants that follow the sun's natural cycle, indoor plants depend entirely on you to provide the right amount of light at the right times.

The good news? Once you understand photoperiods and set up a reliable timer, your grow room essentially runs itself. This guide covers everything from basic light schedules to advanced automation strategies that professional growers use.

Pro tip: Light duration (photoperiod) works together with light intensity (PPFD) to determine your Daily Light Integral (DLI) - the total amount of photosynthetically active light your plants receive each day. Use our grow light calculator to dial in both settings for optimal results.

1. Understanding Photoperiods and DLI

A photoperiod is simply the duration of light exposure in a 24-hour cycle. Plants have evolved to use photoperiod as a signal for seasonal changes, triggering different growth phases based on day length.

The DLI Connection

Daily Light Integral (DLI) measures the total photons your plants receive per day. It's calculated by multiplying light intensity (PPFD) by the number of hours of light:

DLI = PPFD x Hours x 0.0036

This means you can achieve the same DLI with either:

  • High intensity for fewer hours (e.g., 600 PPFD for 12 hours)
  • Lower intensity for more hours (e.g., 400 PPFD for 18 hours)

Important: There's a limit to how much light plants can use at once. Above ~1000-1200 PPFD, most plants experience photoinhibition (diminishing returns). So you can't simply blast plants with extreme light for 6 hours - spreading light over 12-18 hours is more efficient.

Three Types of Photoperiod Response

Type Response Examples
Short-Day Plants Flower when nights are long (>12 hours darkness) Cannabis, chrysanthemums, poinsettias
Long-Day Plants Flower when days are long (>14 hours light) Spinach, lettuce, wheat, barley
Day-Neutral Plants Flower based on age/size, not light duration Tomatoes, peppers, autoflowers, most houseplants

2. Common Light Schedules Explained

Here are the most common light schedules used by indoor growers, with the pros and cons of each:

18/6 (18 Hours On, 6 Hours Off)

The most popular vegetative growth schedule

Pros:

  • Excellent for vegetative growth
  • Allows dark period for plant recovery
  • Lower electricity costs than 24/0
  • Mimics long summer days naturally

Cons:

  • Slightly slower than 20/4 or 24/0
  • Temperature swings during dark period

Best for: Vegetative cannabis, leafy greens, herbs, most indoor plants

20/4 (20 Hours On, 4 Hours Off)

Extended light for faster vegetative growth

Pros:

  • Faster growth than 18/6
  • Still provides dark period
  • Good for autoflowering plants

Cons:

  • Higher electricity costs
  • More heat management needed
  • Some plants may show stress

Best for: Autoflowers, fast vegetative cycles, commercial operations

24/0 (24 Hours On, No Dark Period)

Continuous light - controversial but sometimes used

Pros:

  • Maximum photosynthesis potential
  • Good for seedlings/clones short-term
  • Consistent temperature (no night drop)

Cons:

  • Highest electricity costs
  • Plants may stress from no rest
  • Research shows diminishing returns
  • Can cause nutrient deficiencies

Best for: Seedlings/clones only (short-term), speed-focused commercial grows

12/12 (12 Hours On, 12 Hours Off)

Standard flowering schedule for photoperiod plants

Pros:

  • Triggers flowering in short-day plants
  • Lowest electricity usage
  • Required for flower development

Cons:

  • Slower vegetative growth
  • Requires light-proof dark period
  • Not suitable for all plant types

Best for: Flowering cannabis, photoperiod flowering plants

4. Schedules by Plant Type

Houseplants & Tropicals

Recommended: 12-16 hours
DLI Target: 4-12 mol/m2/day (varies by species)
Notes: Most are day-neutral; schedule for convenience

Common plants: Pothos, Monstera, Philodendron, Ficus, Peace Lily

Succulents & Cacti

Recommended: 12-14 hours
DLI Target: 15-25 mol/m2/day (high light lovers)
Notes: Need higher intensity, not longer duration

See our succulent grow light guide for specific recommendations.

Herbs & Microgreens

Recommended: 14-18 hours
DLI Target: 12-20 mol/m2/day
Notes: Longer light = faster growth and more compact plants

Common plants: Basil, mint, cilantro, parsley, microgreens

Leafy Greens & Lettuce

Recommended: 16-18 hours
DLI Target: 12-17 mol/m2/day
Notes: Avoid <10 hours or lettuce may bolt

Common plants: Lettuce, spinach, kale, arugula, chard

Fruiting Vegetables

Recommended: 14-18 hours (they're day-neutral)
DLI Target: 20-40 mol/m2/day (high light)
Notes: Flower based on age/nutrients, not photoperiod

Common plants: Tomatoes, peppers, cucumbers, strawberries

Cannabis (Photoperiod)

Vegetative: 18/6 or 20/4
Flowering: 12/12 (strict - no light leaks!)
DLI Target: Veg: 25-40, Flower: 40-65 mol/m2/day

See our 4x4 tent guide for optimal setups.

Autoflowering Plants

Recommended: 18/6 or 20/4 (entire lifecycle)
DLI Target: 35-50 mol/m2/day
Notes: Day-neutral genetics; no schedule change needed

Some growers run 24/0, but 20/4 is more cost-effective with similar results.

6. Advanced Automation Tips

Beyond basic on/off scheduling, here are advanced techniques that professional growers use:

Sunrise/Sunset Simulation

Instead of instant on/off, gradually ramp lights up over 15-30 minutes to simulate natural sunrise. This reduces plant stress and allows stomata to open gradually. Some LED controllers and smart dimmers support this natively.

How to implement: Use a dimmable LED driver with a smart dimmer, or chain multiple light bars to separate timers with staggered start times.

Temperature-Based Scheduling

Run lights during cooler nighttime hours to reduce HVAC costs. In hot climates, a "lights out" period during peak afternoon heat (2-6 PM) can save significantly on cooling.

Example schedule: Lights on 6 PM - 12 PM (18 hours), lights off during hottest 6-hour window.

Utility Peak Avoidance

Many utility companies charge higher rates during peak hours (typically 2-7 PM). Schedule your dark period during peak pricing to reduce electricity costs by 20-40%.

Check: Contact your utility or check your bill for "time of use" rate schedules.

Staggered Lighting Zones

For large grows, start lights in different zones 15-30 minutes apart. This prevents massive power spikes that can trip breakers and reduces stress on your electrical system.

Example: Zone 1 at 6:00 AM, Zone 2 at 6:15 AM, Zone 3 at 6:30 AM.

Mid-Day Siesta (Gas Lantern Routine)

Some growers use a "gas lantern routine" (GLR) with a mid-day dark period: 12 hours on / 5.5 hours off / 1 hour on / 5.5 hours off. This can reduce power usage while maintaining vegetative growth, though results are debated.

Note: Not recommended for beginners - stick with proven 18/6 or 12/12 schedules first.

7. Common Timer Mistakes to Avoid

✗

Light Leaks During Dark Period

Even small amounts of light during flowering can cause plants to revert to vegetative growth or develop hermaphrodite traits. Seal all light leaks with tape, use light-proof ducting, and turn off indicator LEDs on equipment.

✗

Inconsistent Scheduling

Manually switching lights leads to inconsistency. Even a few hours of variation can stress plants and reduce yields. Always use a timer - the $12 investment pays for itself many times over.

✗

Overloading Timer Capacity

Running a 1500W load on a 10A timer is a fire hazard. Always check the amp rating (divide watts by voltage: 1500W / 120V = 12.5A) and leave 20% headroom.

✗

Ignoring Daylight Savings Time

Timer schedules don't auto-adjust for DST. A 1-hour shift can interrupt flowering and cause issues. Update timers manually after each DST change, or use smart plugs that adjust automatically.

✗

No Backup Power Plan

Power outages can reset cheap mechanical timers. Use digital timers with battery backup, or note your schedule so you can reprogram quickly after outages.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best grow light schedule for seedlings?

Seedlings do best with 16-18 hours of light per day. This mimics long spring days and promotes healthy growth without stressing young plants. Run lights from 6 AM to midnight, or use a timer for consistent 18/6 scheduling.

Is 24 hours of light better than 18/6?

For most plants, 18/6 (18 hours on, 6 hours off) is better than 24/0 continuous light. Plants use the dark period for important metabolic processes. 24/0 can work for seedlings and clones short-term, but extended use may cause stress and actually slow growth.

How do I trigger flowering with grow lights?

To trigger flowering in photoperiod plants, switch to a 12/12 light schedule (12 hours on, 12 hours off). This mimics fall conditions and signals the plant to transition from vegetative growth to flowering. Maintain complete darkness during the off period.

What timer should I use for grow lights?

Digital timers with 7-day programming are ideal for grow lights. Top picks include the Century 7-Day Digital Timer ($12), BN-LINK 7-Day Heavy Duty Timer ($15), and smart plugs like Kasa or Wemo for remote control. For high-wattage lights (1000W+), use heavy-duty timers rated for your amperage.

Can inconsistent light schedules hurt my plants?

Yes, inconsistent light schedules can stress plants significantly. Irregular timing can cause hermaphroditism in flowering plants, stunted growth, and even revert flowering plants back to vegetative state. Always use a timer rather than manual switching for consistent results.
